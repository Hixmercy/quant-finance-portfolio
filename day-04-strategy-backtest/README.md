# Day 4 – Trading Strategy Backtest (EURUSD)

## 📌 Project Overview
This project builds and evaluates a simple rule-based trading strategy for the EURUSD currency pair using historical price data.

The main purpose of this project is to move beyond market observation and begin testing whether a clear trading rule could have produced useful results over time.

In earlier projects, I explored:
- how EURUSD behaves in terms of returns and drawdowns
- how technical indicators such as moving averages, RSI, and MACD can help interpret market conditions
- how volatility and risk change over time

This project takes the next logical step:

👉 **turning analysis into a trading strategy and testing its historical performance**

---

## 🎯 Objective
The objective of this project is to answer a practical trading question:

**If I follow a simple technical rule consistently, how would that strategy perform over time compared to staying exposed to the market?**

The strategy used here is based on a moving average crossover:

- enter a long position when the 20-day moving average rises above the 50-day moving average
- stay out of the market when the 20-day moving average falls below the 50-day moving average

This creates a simple trend-following framework.

## ⚙️ Strategy Logic
This backtest uses a **simple moving average crossover strategy**.

### Trading Rule
- **Buy / Hold Long:** when `SMA 20 > SMA 50`
- **Exit / Stay Flat:** when `SMA 20 <= SMA 50`

### Why this rule?
The idea behind this approach is that short-term price strength relative to longer-term price behavior may signal a developing upward trend.

When the shorter moving average stays above the longer one, the strategy assumes bullish conditions.
When it drops below, the strategy exits to reduce market exposure.

---

## 🔍 Methodology
The project was implemented in the following steps:

### 1. Load and prepare the EURUSD dataset
The historical data was imported and sorted by date.

### 2. Calculate technical indicators
Two simple moving averages were calculated:
- 20-day moving average
- 50-day moving average

### 3. Create trading signals
A binary signal was generated:
- `1` = in the market
- `0` = out of the market

### 4. Shift the signal
The signal was shifted by one day to avoid **look-ahead bias**.

This is important because it ensures the strategy only acts on information that would have been known at the time.

### 5. Compute strategy returns
Daily strategy returns were calculated by multiplying:
- position
- daily asset return

### 6. Build the equity curve
The cumulative growth of the strategy was tracked over time and compared with a buy-and-hold benchmark.

### 7. Evaluate performance
The strategy was assessed using:
- total return
- annualized return
- annualized volatility
- Sharpe ratio
- maximum drawdown

## 🧠 Analyst Interpretation
This backtest provides an initial view of how a simple trend-following rule performs on EURUSD over time.

The moving average crossover strategy introduces structure into the trading process by defining clear entry and exit conditions. Rather than relying on emotion or discretionary judgment, trades are based on objective market signals.

The strategy-vs-buy-and-hold chart helps show whether the rule adds value over simply staying exposed to the market. In some market conditions, a moving average strategy can help avoid prolonged unfavorable periods by stepping out when trend conditions weaken. However, in sideways or choppy conditions, the strategy may generate false signals and underperform.

The drawdown chart is especially important because it highlights the practical trading experience behind the returns. Even if a strategy is profitable overall, it may still go through uncomfortable periods of loss. Understanding this is essential because traders often abandon strategies not when they stop working completely, but when losses become emotionally difficult to tolerate.

Overall, this project shows that even a simple rule-based approach can be tested and evaluated in a disciplined way. It also shows that profitability alone is not enough — strategy quality must be judged alongside risk, stability, and drawdown behavior.

## 💡 Key Takeaways
- A trading strategy should be based on clear and testable rules
- Backtesting helps evaluate whether an idea had historical value
- Trend-following strategies may perform better in directional markets
- Simple strategies can still experience significant drawdowns
- Risk-adjusted performance matters more than raw return alone
- Structured testing is essential before trusting any strategy in real trading

---

## ⚠️ Limitations
This is a simplified backtest and has important limitations:

- it only uses one basic signal
- it does not include transaction costs or slippage
- it assumes trades can be executed cleanly at the next period
- it focuses only on long exposure
- it does not yet include position sizing or stop-loss logic

Because of this, the results should be interpreted as **research output**, not a production-ready trading system.
